The Ghana economy, the truth about We approaching the end of our studies. What's inline for us

According to the works bank, Ghana’s rapid growth was halted by the COVID-19 pandemic, the March 2020 lockdown, and a sharp decline in commodity exports. The economy had grown at an average of 7 percent in 2017-19, before experiencing a sharp contraction in the second and third quarters of 2020. The economic slowdown had a considerable impact on households. The poverty rate is estimated to have slightly increased from 25 percent in 2019 to 25.5 percent in 2020.

An insight into the data coming up

The overall fiscal deficit doubled to 15.2 percent in 2020. Public debt increased to 81.1 percent of GDP in 2020, placing Ghana at significant risk of debt distress. Growth firmed up further in the first and second quarters of 2021 despite a sharp contraction in mining and the pandemic’s second wave. Provisional fiscal data for the first half of 2021 suggest that the authorities cut spending to make up for revenue shortfalls. The overall fiscal deficit was 5.1 percent of GDP. In simple terms, the. the country is highly indebted and is compelled to cut down costs. Employing graduates will add to the cost burden of the country forcing the country to cut down the number of employees it gives resulting in a high unemployment rate.

The discovery made: Data from the Institute of Statistics, Social and Economic Research (ISSER) of the University of Ghana, revealed that it may take up to 10 years for a large number of graduates to secure employment due to varied challenges that ranged from the lack of employable skills, unavailability of funding capital for entrepreneurship, poor attitudes of graduates towards job opportunities, as well as the low capacities of the industry to absorb the huge numbers. They further argued that only 10 percent of university graduates get employed in the first year of completing school. The International Labor Organization revealed that Unemployment Rate in Ghana increased to 4.50 percent in 2020 from 4.10 percent in 2019 which further dwindles the hope of the graduating student. The statistics are clear, there is a huge imbalance between the number of graduates being released each year and the jobs available in the job market.

Final conclusion

Be keen and alert, but hope on: The National Labour Commission in Ghana , estimates a staggering unemployed graduate figure of 700,000. This represents a serious social waste bearing in mind the private and public investment made in the schooling of those involved. This waste appears all the more reprehensible when at the same time one observes many unfilled vacancies in some skill areas, including teaching in our schools. Another research conducted by Trade Union Congress (TUC) of Ghana revealed that between 250,000 and 300,000 graduates were produced every year by the [more than forty-eight] universities for the job market (Donkor, 2014).It appears that the problem of graduate unemployment has been getting worse for some years now. Truth be told the future doesn't look bright for the graduate, the employment status quo worsens as each year passes fewer individuals are employed into the government sector while the private sector also employs just a few. It dawns on graduates to equipment themselves with expertise which will assist them in becoming entrepreneurs since it seems to be the only option and the only hope for graduates. One great move to begin with, is accepting reality but only few manage to do so.
